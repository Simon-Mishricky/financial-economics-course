## Overview

This repository contains selected course materials from ECON8037: Financial Economics, a graduate-level course taught at the Australian National University. The course provides rigorous training in modern financial theory with applications to asset pricing, portfolio choice, and market equilibrium.

## Course Description

Financial economics describes equilibrium in financial markets where economic agents trade claims to uncertain future payoffs. The course examines how agents should be compensated for:
- **Time**: The time value of money and intertemporal choice
- **Risk**: Uncertainty and risk preferences
- **Liquidity**: Market frictions and trading constraints

## Course Structure

The course is organized into four main modules:

### 1. Introduction to Financial Economics (Weeks 1-2)
- Foundations of financial markets
- Mathematical preliminaries
- Python programming for finance

### 2. Asset Valuation (Weeks 5-8)
- Present value and discounting
- Bond pricing and term structure
- Equity valuation models
- Derivatives pricing fundamentals

### 3. Complete Markets (Weeks 9-11)
- Arrow-Debreu securities
- State-price theory
- Market completeness and spanning
- Fundamental theorems of asset pricing

### 4. Review (Week 12)
- Course synthesis and exam preparation

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course, students are able to:

1. Apply fundamental asset pricing principles to value financial securities
2. Understand the role of arbitrage in determining equilibrium prices
3. Analyze portfolio choice under uncertainty using expected utility theory
4. Work with state-price densities and risk-neutral valuation
5. Implement financial models using computational methods (Python)
6. Critically evaluate empirical evidence on asset pricing theories

## Prerequisites

- **Mathematics**: Calculus, linear algebra, probability theory, optimization
- **Economics**: Microeconomic theory (consumer choice, general equilibrium)

## Key Topics Covered

### Mathematical Foundations
- Probability spaces and random variables
- Expected utility theory
- Stochastic processes
- Dynamic programming

### Asset Pricing Theory
- No-arbitrage pricing
- Risk-neutral valuation
- Stochastic discount factors
- State prices and Arrow-Debreu securities

### Portfolio Theory
- Mean-variance optimization
- Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM)
- Multi-factor models
- Dynamic portfolio choice

### Market Microstructure
- Complete vs. incomplete markets
- Market efficiency
- Information and prices

## Computational Methods

The course incorporates Python programming for:
- Numerical optimization
- Monte Carlo simulation
- Data analysis and visualization
- Implementation of pricing models

Students develop practical skills in translating theoretical models into working code.
